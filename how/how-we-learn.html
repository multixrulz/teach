The net of knowledge

When we learn something, it must be connected in some way to what we already know. This is the big idea of Constructivism. (Constructivism also refers to a teaching method, but it’s flawed).

… everything you know is connected in memory in one giant net, it’s a giant semantic net, and everything has to be connected to something, you don’t have any floating representations in your mind that no one can reach. Even something as weird as a book and a mango, or a helicopter and a screwdriver, you can find a connection, so everything is connected. — Dr. Moshe Bar

I really like the metaphor of a net because:

  • It shows how all knowledge is interconnected, with each piece of knowledge being the interconnections in the net.
  • We can think of the strength and number of interconnections as how well we know something.
  • It lets us visualise narrow and broad thinking <— update when I’ve revisited learning how to learn
  • I extended it to learning new things. Imagine the net catching new knowledge. If there are big gaps in the net, or it hasn’t grown far enough, new ideas just go right past. The bigger the net is, and the tighter it’s interconnected, the more likely it is for the net to catch the new knowledge.
