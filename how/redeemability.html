How to teach

Ensuring absence doesn’t jeopardise education

When students are absent from school, they not only miss one day’s learning — all of the later activities that depended on learning things that day are also affected. Students can start down a vicious cycle of negative thinking and self-jeopardising behaviours if they miss a lot of school and find that they can’t do things. How can we make it possible to recover from an absence?

I don’t yet have a good framework or resources about this. I’ve just done some websearching and noted a few things I thought of myself.

Here are some ideas.

  • Flipped learning — swapping the in-school and homework activities.
  • Students access the “explanation” part of the classroom experience by some method other than by live experience with the teacher. Options include:
    • Reading a book, website, etc.
    • Watching a video (of you, or something you’ve found).
    • Using an online learning website.
    • Reading materials you’ve put together.
  • The in-class activities operate more like a tutorial
  • Put as much as possible online (e.g. class website, Google classroom) so students who were away can find out what happened.
  • Or you could do this physically, with pigeon holes or trays.
  • Gamification can help track who’s done what. It allows more personalised pathways through a course. Allow students to follow their own paths through the course as much as possible, rather than making everyone do the same thing in every lesson.
  • Assigning “Absence buddies”, so each student has someone looking out for them when they’re away. The absence buddy collects handouts etc. and gets the absent student up-to-date on what they missed when they return.
  • Make yourself available outside of scheduled classes, so students can come to you to catch up.
